\begin{abstract}
Reverse logistics disposition decisions---refurbish, harvest components, recycle,
or scrap---must be made under substantial uncertainty about product condition.
Physical inspection resolves this uncertainty but costs \$50--200 per unit and
30--60 technician-minutes, a bottleneck that becomes the binding constraint at
volume. We present a decision-support framework that converts the unstructured
text already accompanying returns (technician notes, maintenance logs, customer
descriptions) into informative priors for a stochastic disposition optimizer.
A modular semantic interface decouples text extraction from optimization: any
classifier satisfying the interface can be substituted without modifying the
downstream engine. An adaptive inspection policy uses extractor confidence to set
inspection depth per asset, skipping physical testing where text evidence is
strong and reserving full testing where it is not. We validate across three
structurally distinct scenarios---enterprise IT decommission, aviation MRO, and
consumer electronics returns---using a conservative keyword extractor as a lower
bound on extraction quality. The complete framework beats the optimization-only
inspect-all baseline in all three scenarios (recovery-value lift 2.9--13.7\%,
paired Wilcoxon $p{<}0.001$, 30~seeds) while saving \$628--\$23,688 per batch in
inspection cost. Calibration rises monotonically with extractor quality on the
same notes: a brittle keyword matcher gives Pearson $r{=}0.47/0.32/0.36$ across
scenarios, a full-vocabulary phrase matcher $0.73/0.54/0.74$, and a deployed LLM
(DeepSeek) $0.81/0.83/0.78$---demonstrating the extractor-agnostic claim with
three real points, all consumed through one interface. All results reproduce from
the released Reverse Logistics Decision Benchmark (RLDB).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Resource recovery through reverse logistics is a large and growing operational
challenge. The global data-center IT asset disposition market reached \$11.4B in
2023~\cite{GlobalMktInsights}, yet returned products are routinely routed to
suboptimal paths because condition assessment depends on physical inspection that
does not scale with volume. Aviation MRO faces the same pressure (annual market
demand exceeds \$80B~\cite{OliverWyman}), and U.S.\ retail returns reached \$743B
in 2023 at a 14.5\% return rate~\cite{NRF}, most processed with no condition-based
routing.

The core problem is an information gap. Decision-makers must choose among
recovery options---refurbish, harvest components, recycle, or scrap---with little
knowledge of actual condition. Physical inspection closes that gap but is
expensive and slow. The textual data already accompanying most returns (technician
notes, maintenance logs, customer-reported symptoms) often contains
condition-relevant signals, yet existing optimization frameworks require
structured numerical inputs and cannot use
them~\cite{Godichaud2022,Govindan2015}.

This paper formalizes the integration of semantic signals from unstructured text
into stochastic disposition optimization. The key insight is that text, when
extracted and calibrated, acts as a noisy but informative prior on product
condition. That prior parameterizes Beta-distributed yield models the decision
engine uses to rank and allocate assets under capacity constraints.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Validation overview.}
Three scenarios, chosen to differ structurally in text type, regulatory
environment, and economic scale:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep]
\item \textbf{S1 (IT Infrastructure):} Planned decommissions, descriptive
  technician notes. Calibration climbs keyword $0.47$ $\rightarrow$ phrase-matcher
  $0.73$ $\rightarrow$ LLM $0.81$. The combined framework lifts TRV 13.7\% over
  Opt-only; the adaptive inspection policy alone (without the optimizer)
  contributes 3.3\% independently.

\item \textbf{S2 (Aviation MRO):} FAA Part~145 mandates, standardized
  maintenance codes, $r{=}0.32$. Text + adaptive inspection without the optimizer
  destroys \$188K vs.\ Opt-only; the optimizer recovers \$233K, leaving a net
  $+$2.9\% lift. A structured-feature baseline with full inspection outperforms on
  absolute TRV because the text signal is insufficient to cover the cost of
  reduced inspection coverage.

\item \textbf{S3 (Consumer Electronics):} High-volume returns, $r{=}0.36$. The
  framework achieves $+$4.5\% TRV over Opt-only at slightly lower demand coverage
  (93.8\% vs.\ 96.0\%), because the optimizer correctly scraps marginal assets
  that Opt-only over-processes.
\end{itemize}

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item A framework for integrating semantic text signals into stochastic reverse
  logistics disposition models, with clean separation between extraction and
  optimization.
\item An adaptive inspection policy that sets inspection depth from extractor
  confidence---effective as a standalone component in high-signal domains,
  and requiring the optimizer in low-signal domains.
\item A three-scenario simulation study with a calibration ladder of three real
  extractors (keyword, full-vocabulary phrase matcher, and a deployed LLM),
  establishing where text signals add value, where they are advisory, and where
  they enable volume triage. Code and benchmark are fully released.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}

Reverse logistics decisions are sequential and partially irreversible.
Mixed-integer programming for disassembly lot-sizing~\cite{Godichaud2022} and
stochastic programming for closed-loop network design~\cite{Govindan2015} are
the classical approaches, but both require structured numerical inputs specified
before optimization. In practice, much decision-relevant information lives in
unstructured narratives that no existing framework can ingest without manual
translation.

Text-based methods have advanced in forward supply chain settings.
OptiGuide~\cite{Yang2023} translates natural language into optimization code;
InvAgent~\cite{Quan2024} coordinates multi-agent inventory decisions;
AlMahri et al.~\cite{AlMahri2024} extract supply chain relationships via
zero-shot learning. None addresses reverse logistics disposition under yield
uncertainty. Table~\ref{tab:positioning} shows the gap this work fills.

\section{Framework Design}

\subsection{Problem Setting}

Assets arrive in periodic batches. Each asset $m$ has structured attributes
(type, age, BOM structure) and unstructured context (inspection notes
$\mathcal{T}(m)$, site history $H(s(m))$). The BOM has three levels: $L_0$
(whole unit), $L_1$ (major assemblies), $L_2$ (components); moving from $L_1$
to $L_2$ costs 3--5$\times$ more due to precision tooling requirements.
Per asset, the system makes two decisions: $D_0$ (triage: accept or scrap) and
$D_2$ (disposition: disassembly depth and allocation). Expected recovery value at
level $l$ is:
\begin{equation}
V(m,l) = \sum_{c\in\text{BOM}(m,l)} p_c\cdot\mathbb{E}[Y_c(m)] - C_l
\label{eq:value}
\end{equation}
where $p_c$ is component market price, $C_l$ is disassembly cost to level $l$,
and $Y_c(m)\sim\text{Beta}(\alpha_c,\beta_c)$ is stochastic component yield.

\subsection{System Architecture}

The framework has two layers (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}). The \emph{Semantic Extraction
Layer} maps unstructured text to a context factor $\phi\in(0,1]$ and confidence
score $\sigma\in[0,1]$. The \emph{Recovery Decision Engine} evaluates
Eq.~(\ref{eq:value}) via $N{=}1000$ Monte Carlo draws, ranks assets by expected
net value, and allocates greedily under capacity constraints.

\subsection{Context Extraction Layer}

The framework defines a semantic interface: any extractor returning
$\phi\in(0,1]$ and $\sigma\in[0,1]$ plugs in without changing the inspection
policy or decision engine. The interface is the architectural contribution;
the extraction mechanism is modular.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Experimental extractor.}
For full reproducibility and to isolate policy gains from extraction quality,
we use a deterministic keyword-and-pattern classifier with vocabularies drawn
from iFixit teardown reports (S1), ATA-coded SDR language (S2), and consumer
return phrasing (S3). When no vocabulary signal fires, the classifier falls back
to $\phi{=}1.0$ (uninformative prior), triggering full inspection. This is a
deliberate lower bound.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Reference extractors for calibration.}
We compare the keyword classifier against two stronger readers of the same noisy,
decoupled note (Table~\ref{tab:calibration}): a deterministic phrase matcher that
covers the complete condition vocabulary per scenario and maps it to a
recoverability estimate via a table fixed before extraction, and an optional LLM
(Section~\ref{sec:calibration}). None sees the ground-truth condition label, and
all are bounded below $r{=}1.0$ by the note generation noise
($p_\text{omit}{=}0.15$, $p_\text{mislabel}{=}0.25$). The phrase matcher emits one
discrete $\phi$ per condition; the LLM emits a graded $\phi$ and, as
Section~\ref{sec:calibration} shows, reads the notes more accurately.

The context factor $\phi(m)$ adjusts baseline yield downward when negative
signals are present; positive signals cannot push $\phi$ above 1.0. Multiple
negative signals combine multiplicatively.

\subsection{Prior-Adjustment Mechanism}\label{sec:prior}

Given $\phi$ and component baseline yield $\hat{y}_c$, the Beta prior is
shifted by moment matching:
\begin{equation}
\alpha'_c = \phi(m)\cdot\hat{y}_c\cdot\kappa,\quad
\beta'_c  = (1-\phi(m)\cdot\hat{y}_c)\cdot\kappa
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ is the effective prior sample size. This is a deterministic
pseudo-count encoding text-derived belief; there is no likelihood function or
posterior computation.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Key insight on $\kappa$.}
$\kappa$ scales $\alpha'_c$ and $\beta'_c$ but leaves the Beta mean
$\phi\cdot\hat{y}_c$ invariant. Since the decision engine ranks assets by that
MC mean, $\kappa$ does not change which assets get processed or how they are
ranked. Sweeping $\kappa\in\{5,10,20,40,60,80\}$ on S1 confirms this:
spread is 0.86 percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:kappa}). Framework value
comes from \emph{inspection targeting}, not prior concentration. We use
$\kappa{=}10$.

\subsection{Adaptive Inspection Policy}

The system uses confidence $\sigma(m)$ to set inspection depth per asset:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item $\sigma\geq\tau_h$: skip inspection, use $\phi$ directly;
\item $\tau_l\leq\sigma<\tau_h$: quick functional test;
\item $\sigma<\tau_l$: full component-level inspection.
\end{itemize}
S1/S2 use $\tau_h{=}0.5$, $\tau_l{=}0.25$ (aggressive skip); S3 uses
$\tau_l{=}0.45$ because colloquial text rarely achieves high confidence.
Each skipped inspection saves \$50--200 and 30--60 minutes per asset. Wrong skips
are recoverable: the asset goes to scrap rather than a missed recovery, bounded
by its realized value.

\subsection{Decision Engine}

The engine processes weekly batches (daily for S3). For each asset it evaluates
$V(m,l)$ at each disassembly level via MC sampling, ranks by expected net value,
and allocates greedily subject to processing capacity. Under tight capacity,
only the highest-value assets are processed; selective harvesting behavior
emerges directly from the capacity constraint without explicit priority rules.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Decoupled note generation.}
In all three scenarios the technician note or review text is generated from an
independent noisy observation of the ground-truth condition, not from the
condition that determines true yield. Corruption modes: omission ($p{=}0.15$,
note becomes uninformative) and severity mislabeling ($p{=}0.25$, perceived
severity drifts one adjacent category). Any extractor--yield correlation is
therefore earned through genuine signal recovery, not baked in by construction.
See \texttt{src/data\_generators/noise.py} in the RLDB.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Baselines.}
(1)~\emph{Random}---random $L_2$/$L_1$/scrap assignment (lower bound);
(2)~\emph{Rule-based FIFO}---whole-unit processing in arrival order;
(3)~\emph{XGBoost proxy}---greedy optimizer, $\phi$ from age bracket only,
learned on a held-out population (seed~99999, $N{=}2000$);
(4)~\emph{Opt-only}---$\phi{=}1.0$, full inspection, greedy optimizer
(primary comparison); (5)~\emph{Semantic-only}---text signals, threshold
routing, no optimizer.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Metrics.}
\emph{TRV}: net dollars after all processing, inspection, and disposal costs.
\emph{DCR}: fraction of incoming assets routed to a recovery pathway.
\emph{ICS}: dollars saved relative to inspecting every asset at full depth.
ICS for non-adaptive baselines (Random, XGBoost, Opt-only) reflects only
capacity-scrapping savings. The inspection saving \emph{attributable to the
adaptive policy} is $\Delta\text{ICS} = \text{ICS}_\text{Ours} -
\text{ICS}_\text{Opt-only}$: \$23,688 (S1), \$10,167 (S2), \$628 (S3).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{S1: IT Infrastructure.}
The \$73K TRV lift over Opt-only ($+13.7\%$) separates cleanly into two sources.
The adaptive inspection policy alone (Sem.-only, no optimizer) adds \$18K
($+3.3\%$); it also saves \$32K in inspection (ICS), \$24K more than Opt-only's
\$8K, by skipping physical tests where the text signal is strong. The greedy
optimizer on top adds another \$55K by routing assets to the right recovery level.
Both components contribute independently. Full demand coverage (DCR 100\%) shows
that an $r{=}0.47$ text signal is sufficient for the optimizer to route every
asset to a positive-value recovery path.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{S2: Aviation MRO.}
The value decomposition here differs structurally from S1. Sem.-only (text +
adaptive inspection, no optimizer) destroys \$188K vs.\ Opt-only because
threshold routing over-processes low-value assets without capacity-aware ranking.
The optimizer recovers \$233K of that, leaving a net $+$\$45K ($+2.9\%$). XGBoost
outperforms Ours in absolute TRV (\$1,678K vs.\ \$1,610K): combining a
calibrated prior ($\phi{\approx}0.55$) with full inspection avoids any coverage
gap. At $r{=}0.32$ text calibration, the savings from the adaptive policy do not
fully offset the recoveries occasionally missed via wrong skips. In a
Part~145-regulated domain this advisory mode is correct: text directs triage
effort but does not replace mandated inspection authority.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{S3: Consumer Electronics.}
Sem.-only produces negative TRV ($-$\$14K) because inspection costs exceed
recovery value for the assets threshold routing selects. Ours reaches
$+$\$3K ($+4.5\%$) over Opt-only by combining text routing with the optimizer.
Ours achieves this at \emph{lower} DCR than Opt-only (93.8\% vs.\ 96.0\%):
when $\phi$ falls below the breakeven point, the optimizer scraps the asset
rather than processing it at a loss. Lower DCR is correct selective behavior,
not degraded coverage.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Statistical note.}
Randomness enters only through Monte Carlo yield sampling and seed-varied asset
populations. $p{<}0.001$ over 30 seeds measures stability of these effects,
not generalization to held-out datasets or to a different extractor.

The three extractors form a monotone ladder on the same decoupled notes---keyword
(conservative) below the full-vocabulary phrase matcher below the LLM---with no
change to the decision architecture. This is the extractor-agnostic property made
concrete: calibration rises with extractor quality from a brittle keyword matcher
($r{=}0.32$--$0.47$) through a complete phrase matcher ($0.54$--$0.74$) to a
deployed LLM ($0.78$--$0.83$), and the framework consumes any of them through one
interface. (Keyword fallback rates---notes where no vocabulary signal fires and
$\phi{\gets}1.0$---are 28/43/55\% for S1/S2/S3, which explains the ordering of the
keyword column: more colloquial or abbreviated text yields more uninformative
notes.)

Two points deserve care. First, the LLM exceeds the phrase matcher in every
scenario, so the phrase matcher is \emph{not} a true ceiling: it assigns one
discrete $\phi$ per recognized condition and misreads phrasing variants, whereas
the LLM recovers the described condition more robustly and emits a graded $\phi$
that tracks recovery probability more closely. Second, the largest gain is on S2
(0.54 to 0.83). We had expected S2 to be hardest because standardized aviation
codes can discretize condition into coarse bins; the LLM result shows that our
synthetic SDR notes, which are descriptive narratives rather than bare codes,
still carry recoverable ordinal signal. The low keyword and phrase-matcher $r$ on
S2 therefore reflect those extractors' limits, not an information ceiling of the
text---we state this plainly rather than the stronger claim of an earlier draft.
Real ATA-coded fields, as opposed to descriptive SDR narratives, remain a separate
and untested case. The LLM column is excluded from the seeded simulation because
its output is not bit-reproducible; the scored responses are cached in the
benchmark, so the reported $r$ reproduces exactly without re-paying API cost.

\subsection{Error Analysis and Case Studies}

\noindent\textbf{Error analysis.}
The failure mode of interest is \emph{overestimation}: the policy skips
inspection on high confidence ($\sigma{\geq}\tau_h$) while the extractor reports a
high context factor ($\phi{>}0.7$), yet the asset's true yield is low
($<0.30$)---a confidently-kept bad unit that was never physically checked. Over
30 seeds the rate is 1.6\% in S1, 2.5\% in S2, and 0.0\% in S3 (computed by
\texttt{src/s2s} over the asset populations; reproducible). The rate is highest in
S2: with weak text calibration ($r{=}0.32$) the keyword extractor more often reads
a degraded unit as serviceable, which is the concrete reason aviation must run in
advisory mode with mandated inspection retained. The S3 rate is essentially zero
because consumer language rarely yields a confident-but-wrong ``good'' reading.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Case 1: Correct skip (S1).}
Note: ``\textit{Routine decommission. All components seated properly. No
corrosion.}'' The keyword extractor returns $\phi{\approx}0.93$,
$\sigma{\approx}0.88$ (mean over RNG draws). High confidence skips the \$75 L2
inspection; the high $\phi$ sends the unit to component harvest. Inspection
targeting acting on a genuine signal.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Case 2: Correct flag (S1).}
Note: ``\textit{PSU failure. Visible burn marks on mainboard near power connector
J12. CPU smells burnt.}'' The extractor returns $\phi{\approx}0.39$,
$\sigma{\approx}0.87$. Confidence is high, so inspection is skipped---but the low
$\phi$ routes the unit to scrap, avoiding wasted disassembly. Confidence
($\sigma$) and condition ($\phi$) are independent axes: the model is confident the
unit is damaged. These two cases are reproducible traces of the keyword extractor
on benchmark template notes, not hand-picked illustrations.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

\noindent\textbf{Capacity constraints.}
Halving processing capacity (50\% of baseline) reduces Ours TRV by 32\% in S2 and
26\% in S3 but only 3\% in S1 (30 seeds, \texttt{capacity\_fraction=0.5}). The
difference is the per-asset processing cost: S2's $L_2$ overhaul (150~min) and
S3's high volume make capacity the binding constraint, whereas S1's lighter
disassembly (32~min) leaves slack, so the value-ranked allocator still captures
nearly all recoverable value at half capacity. Selective harvesting under a
binding constraint is exactly the intended behavior.

\noindent\textbf{Confidence thresholds.}
We use $\tau_h{=}0.5$, $\tau_l{=}0.25$ for the high-value scenarios (S1, S2) and a
narrower gap ($\tau_l{=}0.45$) for S3, set from a cost argument rather than tuned
to the results: a wrong skip costs at most the asset's realized value, which for
high-value assets is usually less than paying \$75--100 to inspect every unit, so
an aggressive skip threshold is justified; colloquial S3 text rarely earns high
confidence, so S3 needs a tighter band.

\noindent\textbf{$\kappa$ sensitivity.}
Table~\ref{tab:kappa} confirms that $\kappa$ does not change which assets get
processed (the Beta mean is $\kappa$-invariant). The near-flat profile validates
the central claim: framework value comes from inspection targeting, not from
prior concentration.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The framework requires only a greedy MC allocator and any extractor satisfying a
lightweight two-output interface. Adapting to a new domain means updating the
extraction vocabulary and recalibrating the $\phi$-to-yield mapping, not
retraining a model. The strongest deployment case is where text already exists as
part of normal operations (IT decommission work orders, MRO records) and
inspection is the binding resource. In high-volume, low-margin settings the
triage framing is the contribution: routing returns to the right quality tier
without inspecting every unit.

\subsection{Limitations}

All simulation data is synthetic, calibrated from real sources; organic notes
from actual operations may carry noise that requires extractor recalibration, and
the real-world case of bare ATA-coded fields (rather than the descriptive SDR
narratives modeled here) is untested. Even the low S2 overestimation rate (2.5\%)
is unacceptable for autonomous operation in a safety-critical domain, so the
framework there should direct triage and inspection effort, not replace mandated
physical inspection. The LLM extractor was evaluated for calibration only; running
it through the full seeded TRV pipeline---and thus testing whether higher
calibration translates into higher recovery value---is left to future work because
LLM output is not bit-reproducible. The optimization is single-period;
multi-period dynamics with inventory carry-over remain open. Finally, the
$p{<}0.001$ result measures simulation stability across seeds, not generalization
across datasets or extractors.

\subsection{RLDB Benchmark and Reproducibility}

The Reverse Logistics Decision Benchmark (RLDB) contains: scenario
configurations for all three settings; stochastic generators with decoupled
note--condition generation; scripts that reproduce every table without manual
steps; and all five baseline implementations. Key commands:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item \texttt{run\_summary.py --seeds 0-29} $\rightarrow$ Table~\ref{tab:main}
\item \texttt{run\_calibration.py --seeds 0-29} $\rightarrow$ Table~\ref{tab:calibration}
\item \texttt{run\_kappa\_sweep.py --seeds 0-29} $\rightarrow$ Table~\ref{tab:kappa}
\end{itemize}

The benchmark ships LLM extractors (Anthropic Claude, Google Gemini, and DeepSeek)
behind the same interface, invoked with \texttt{run\_calibration.py --llm}. The
LLM column of Table~\ref{tab:calibration} was produced this way with
\texttt{deepseek-chat}; the scored responses are cached in the repository, so the
reported $r$ values reproduce exactly with no API key or further cost. A reviewer
with a key can rerun against any supported provider.

All code, configurations, and calibration sources are released under the MIT
License at
\href{https://github.com/shuan63/reverse_logistics}{github.com/shuan63/reverse\_logistics}.
No proprietary data is used; all synthetic data is generated deterministically
from published seeds, so every table reproduces exactly.

\section{Conclusion}

The central finding is that unstructured return text, extracted with even a
conservative keyword classifier, contains enough signal to reduce inspection
cost while matching or exceeding the recovery value of an inspect-every-asset
baseline. The mechanism is inspection targeting: by skipping inspection on
high-confidence assets and reserving full physical testing for uncertain ones,
the framework achieves the same economic output at lower cost.

How much value the framework delivers depends on how informative the text is.
In IT infrastructure decommission ($r{=}0.47$), the 13.7\% TRV lift over
Opt-only is the largest, and the adaptive inspection policy alone adds 3.3\%
before the optimizer is applied---both components are independently useful.
In aviation MRO ($r{=}0.32$), the framework is advisory: it saves inspection
cost and outperforms Opt-only, but a structured-feature baseline with full
inspection achieves higher absolute TRV because the text signal cannot compensate
for reduced coverage. In consumer electronics, where per-unit inspection is
rarely economic regardless, the contribution is categorical triage at volume.

The calibration ladder in Table~\ref{tab:calibration} quantifies the extraction
headroom directly. On the same notes, calibration climbs from the brittle keyword
matcher ($r{=}0.32$--$0.47$) to a deployed LLM ($r{=}0.78$--$0.83$) with no change
to the decision architecture. Every recovery number in this paper is therefore a
floor set by the deliberately conservative keyword extractor; a better reader
plugs into the same interface and raises calibration measurably, which is the
operational meaning of the extractor-agnostic design.
