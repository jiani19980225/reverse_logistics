\begin{abstract}
Disposition decisions in reverse logistics pipelines must be made under real
uncertainty about product condition. Whether to refurbish, harvest components,
recycle, or scrap a returned asset typically gets resolved through physical
inspection alone, at \$50--200 per unit and 30--60 technician-minutes. At
volume that cost becomes the binding constraint.

We describe a decision-support framework that converts the unstructured text
already accompanying returns (technician notes, maintenance logs, customer
descriptions) into informative priors for a stochastic disposition optimizer.
A semantic interface decouples text extraction from decision optimization: any
classifier satisfying the interface can plug in without touching the downstream
engine. An adaptive inspection policy then uses extractor confidence to set
inspection depth per asset, skipping physical testing where text evidence is
strong and falling back to full component-level inspection when it is not.

We validate across three structurally distinct scenarios (enterprise IT
decommission, aviation MRO, consumer electronics returns) using a conservative
keyword extractor as a lower bound on extraction quality. The full framework
(text extraction + adaptive inspection + greedy optimizer) beats the
optimization-only inspect-all baseline (Opt-only) in all three scenarios
(TRV lift 2.9--13.7\% vs.\ Opt-only, paired Wilcoxon $p<0.001$, 30~seeds)
while saving \$628--\$23,688 per batch in inspection cost. In S1, the adaptive
inspection policy alone (without the full optimizer) already exceeds Opt-only
by 3.3\%, so both components contribute independently. In S2, a structured-age
baseline with full inspection outperforms the framework on absolute TRV because
the text signal is too weak to cover the inspection cost reduction; this is an
expected result given the weak keyword calibration ($r=0.32$) in that domain.

Keyword-extractor calibration on the decoupled synthetic benchmark gives
Pearson $r=0.47$ for descriptive IT notes, $r=0.32$ for standardized aviation
codes, and $r=0.36$ for colloquial consumer text. A deterministic phrase-matching
reader with complete note-vocabulary coverage reaches $r=0.73$, $r=0.54$, and
$r=0.74$ on the same benchmark, bounding what any classifier reading the same
noisy notes could achieve without seeing ground-truth condition labels. All
results reproduce from the released Reverse Logistics Decision Benchmark (RLDB).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Resource recovery through reverse logistics is a growing operational problem.
The global IT asset disposition market reached \$11.4B in~2023~\cite{GlobalMktInsights},
yet disposition accuracy remains low: returned products land in suboptimal
recovery paths because condition assessment depends on physical inspection that
does not scale with volume. Aviation MRO faces the same pressure, with annual
market demand exceeding \$80B~\cite{OliverWyman}; even modest reductions in
unnecessary inspection have material cost impact. Consumer electronics returns
exceed 3.6 billion units annually~\cite{NRF}, most processed with no
condition-based routing at all.

The underlying problem is an information gap. Decision-makers must choose among
recovery options with little knowledge of actual product condition. Physical
inspection closes that gap but is expensive and slow. The textual data that
already accompanies most returns (technician notes, maintenance logs,
customer-reported symptoms) often contains condition-relevant signals, yet
existing optimization frameworks cannot use them: they require structured
numerical inputs prepared before optimization begins~\cite{Godichaud2022,Govindan2015}.

This paper formalizes the integration of semantic signals from unstructured text
into stochastic disposition optimization. The core idea is simple: text,
extracted and calibrated, acts as a noisy but useful prior on product condition.
That prior parameterizes Beta-distributed yield models the decision engine uses
to rank and allocate assets under capacity constraints.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Validation.}
We evaluate across three scenarios chosen to differ structurally:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{S1 (IT Infrastructure):} Planned decommissions with descriptive
technician notes ($r=0.47$; phrase-matching ceiling $r=0.73$). The combined
framework lifts TRV 13.7\% over Opt-only, and the adaptive inspection policy
alone (without the full optimizer) adds 3.3\% -- so both components contribute
value independently.

\item \textbf{S2 (Aviation MRO):} Safety-critical domain, FAA Part~145
inspection mandates, standardized codes ($r=0.32$). Text + inspection policy
alone \emph{hurts} TRV in S2 (threshold routing without optimization destroys
\$188K vs.\ Opt-only); the optimizer recovers \$233K of that, leaving a net
+2.9\% lift over Opt-only. A structured-feature baseline with full inspection
outperforms on absolute TRV, showing that when the text signal is this weak,
inspection savings cannot compensate for coverage reduction.

\item \textbf{S3 (Consumer Electronics):} High-volume returns where per-unit
inspection is rarely economic ($r=0.36$). Ours achieves +4.5\% TRV over
Opt-only at slightly lower demand coverage (93.8\% vs.\ 96.0\%), because the
text signal correctly identifies marginal assets as unprofitable and scraps them
rather than processing them at a loss.
\end{itemize}

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A framework for integrating semantic signals from unstructured text into
stochastic reverse logistics disposition models, with a clean separation between
extraction and optimization.
\item An adaptive inspection policy that sets inspection depth per asset from
extractor confidence. In high-signal settings (S1), the policy alone adds
measurable TRV over the inspect-all baseline. In weak-signal settings (S2, S3),
it requires the optimizer to capture net positive value.
\item A three-scenario simulation study with explicit bounds on extraction
quality, establishing where text signals add value, where they are advisory,
and where they enable volume triage. Code and benchmark are released.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Reverse Logistics Decision Challenges}

Reverse logistics decisions are sequential and partially irreversible. Classical
planning approaches include mixed-integer programming for disassembly lot-sizing
under lead time uncertainty~\cite{Godichaud2022} and stochastic programming for
closed-loop network design~\cite{Govindan2015}. These methods assume structured
numerical inputs. In practice, much of the decision-relevant information lives
in unstructured sources that no existing framework can read without manual
translation.

\subsection{Text-Based Condition Assessment}

generates optimization code from natural language; InvAgent~\cite{Quan2024}

extract supply chain relationships with zero-shot learning. None of this work

\section{Framework Design}

\subsection{Problem Setting}

Assets arrive in periodic batches for disposition. Each asset $m$ has structured
attributes (type, age, source site, BOM structure) and unstructured context
(inspection notes $\mathcal{T}(m)$, site history $H(s(m))$, active business
directives). The BOM is a three-level hierarchy: $L_0$ (whole unit), $L_1$
(major assemblies), $L_2$ (individual components). Moving from $L_1$ to $L_2$
costs 3--5$\times$ more than $L_0$ to $L_1$ because socketed component
extraction requires precision tooling.

Per asset, two decisions are made: $D_0$ (triage: accept for processing or scrap
as-is) and $D_2$ (disposition: disassembly depth and component allocation).
The expected recovery value at disassembly level $l$ is:
\begin{equation}
V(m, l) = \sum_{c \in \mathrm{BOM}(m,l)} p_c \cdot \mathbb{E}[Y_c(m)] - C_l
\label{eq:value}
\end{equation}
where $p_c$ is the component market price, $C_l$ is the disassembly cost to
level $l$, and $Y_c(m) \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_c, \beta_c)$ is the stochastic
component yield.

\subsection{System Architecture}

The framework has two layers. The \emph{Semantic Extraction Layer} converts
unstructured text into a context factor $\phi \in (0,1]$ and a confidence score
$\sigma \in [0,1]$. The \emph{Recovery Decision Engine} evaluates
Eq.~(\ref{eq:value}) at each feasible disassembly level via Monte Carlo
sampling ($N=1000$ draws), ranks assets by expected net value, and allocates
greedily to demand streams under capacity constraints.

\subsection{Context Extraction Layer}

The framework defines a semantic interface that decouples text extraction from
decision optimization. Any extractor returning $\phi \in (0,1]$ and
$\sigma \in [0,1]$ can substitute without modifying the inspection policy or
decision engine. The interface is the contribution; the specific extraction
mechanism is a plug-in.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Experimental extractor.}
We use a deterministic keyword-and-pattern classifier with vocabularies drawn
from iFixit teardown reports (S1), ATA-coded SDR language (S2), and consumer
return phrasing (S3). The classifier is conservative by design: it falls back
to the uninformative prior ($\phi=1.0$) on any note for which no vocabulary
signal fires. Calibration on the decoupled synthetic benchmark
(Section~\ref{sec:calibration}) gives $r=0.47$ on S1, $r=0.32$ on S2, and
$r=0.36$ on S3.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Full-vocabulary reference extractor.}
To bound what better extraction could achieve on the same benchmark, we
implement a second deterministic phrase matcher (see Table~\ref{tab:calibration})
that covers the complete condition vocabulary for each scenario and maps the
described condition to a recoverability estimate via a table fixed before
extraction. It reads the same noisy, decoupled note as the keyword classifier
and cannot see the ground-truth condition. Neither extractor is an oracle. The
reason both stay below $r=1.0$ is that the note itself is a lossy signal: the
imposed generation noise ($p_\text{omit}=0.15$, $p_\text{mislabel}=0.25$) sets
a ceiling that no phrase-matching classifier reading only the note can exceed.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Extractor choice rationale.}
We pick a keyword classifier for three reasons: (1)~full reproducibility without
API or model-versioning constraints; (2)~performance gains trace to the adaptive
policy rather than extraction quality; and (3)~the classifier's known failure
modes (it cannot parse negation, implicit context, or domain abbreviations)
stress-test the policy's tolerance for noisy inputs. Any capable extractor
satisfying the interface would improve calibration; the architecture requires
no change.

The context factor $\phi(m)$ adjusts baseline yield downward when negative
signals are present. Positive signals do not push yield above baseline
($\phi \leq 1.0$). Multiple negative signals combine multiplicatively. On
extraction failure the mechanism defaults to $\phi=1.0$, which triggers full
inspection via the adaptive policy.

\subsection{Prior-Adjustment Mechanism}
\label{sec:framework-prior}

Given $\phi$ and baseline yield $\hat{y}_c$ for component $c$, the Beta prior
is adjusted by moment matching:
\begin{equation}
\alpha'_c = \phi(m) \cdot \hat{y}_c \cdot \kappa, \quad
\beta'_c  = (1 - \phi(m) \cdot \hat{y}_c) \cdot \kappa
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ controls the effective prior sample size. This is a deterministic
shift of the prior mean toward the extractor's point estimate with strength
proportional to source reliability. There is no likelihood function and no
posterior computation; the mechanism is analogous to setting a pseudo-count
that encodes text-derived belief.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Choice of $\kappa$ and what it tells us.}
We use $\kappa=10$ as a practical default. The key insight is that $\kappa$
does not change \emph{which assets get processed}: it scales $\alpha'_c$ and
$\beta'_c$ proportionally but leaves the Beta mean ($\phi \cdot \hat{y}_c$)
invariant, and the decision engine ranks assets by that mean. Sweeping $\kappa
\in \{5,10,20,40,60,80\}$ on S1 confirms this analytically (spread: 0.86
percentage points, Table~\ref{tab:kappa}). This near-flatness is not a
limitation; it reveals that the framework's economic value comes from
\emph{inspection targeting}, not from how confidently the text signal
concentrates the yield prior. Getting the right assets to the right inspection
tier is the mechanism.

\subsection{Adaptive Inspection Policy}

Instead of uniform inspection across every asset, the system uses confidence
$\sigma(m)$ to set inspection depth:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\sigma \geq \tau_h$: skip inspection, use $\phi$ directly;
\item $\tau_l \leq \sigma < \tau_h$: quick functional test;
\item $\sigma < \tau_l$: full component-level inspection.
\end{itemize}
$\tau_h=0.5$ and $\tau_l=0.25$ work well for high-value assets (S1, S2);
S3 uses $\tau_l=0.45$ because colloquial text rarely produces high confidence.
Each skipped inspection saves \$50--200 and 30--60 minutes. Each wrong skip is
a recoverable error: the asset goes to scrap rather than a missed recovery,
and the scrap penalty is bounded by the asset's realized value.

\subsection{Decision Engine}

The Recovery Decision Engine processes weekly batches (daily for S3). For each
asset it evaluates $V(m,l)$ via Monte Carlo at each feasible disassembly level,
ranks by expected net value, and allocates greedily subject to processing
capacity. Under tight capacity, only the highest-value assets get processed;
the rest are scrapped. This selective behavior emerges directly from the
capacity constraint, requiring no explicit priority rules.

\subsection{Data Sources and Generation}

All simulation data is synthetic. Parameters (prices, yield rates, costs) are
calibrated from the public sources in Table~\ref{tab:datasources}; the
simulation output is not adjusted to hit any target.

A critical design property is \emph{decoupled note generation}. In all three
scenarios, the technician note or review text is generated from an independent
noisy observation of ground-truth condition, not from the condition that sets
true yield. Two corruption modes: omission (note becomes uninformative,
$p=0.15$) and severity mislabeling (perceived severity drifts by one adjacent
category, $p=0.25$). Any extractor--yield correlation in the results therefore
comes from genuine signal recovery, not from a construction artifact. See
\texttt{src/data\_generators/noise.py} in the RLDB.

\subsection{Baselines and Metrics}

We compare against five baselines: (1)~\emph{Random disposition}---random
$L_2$/$L_1$/scrap assignment, no ranking (lower bound); (2)~\emph{Rule-based
FIFO}---whole-unit processing in arrival order, no disassembly optimization;
(3)~\emph{XGBoost proxy}---greedy optimizer with $\phi$ from age bracket only,
learned on a held-out training population (seed 99999, $N=2000$) independent
of all evaluation seeds; (4)~\emph{Optimization-only} ($\phi=1.0$, full
inspection)---the primary comparison baseline; and (5)~\emph{Semantic-only}---
text signals with threshold-based routing only, no optimizer.

Three metrics: \emph{Total Recovery Value} (TRV), net dollars after all
processing, inspection, and disposal costs; \emph{Demand Coverage Rate} (DCR),
fraction of incoming assets routed to a recovery pathway; and \emph{Inspection
Cost Savings} (ICS), total dollars saved relative to inspecting every asset at
full depth. Note that ICS for non-adaptive baselines (Random, XGBoost, Opt-only)
reflects only capacity-scrapping savings, not inspection policy savings. The
incremental inspection saving attributable to the adaptive policy is
$\mathrm{ICS}_\text{Ours} - \mathrm{ICS}_\text{Opt-only}$: \$23,688 (S1),
\$10,167 (S2), and \$628 (S3).

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{S1.}
The \$73K TRV advantage over Opt-only (+13.7\%) has two separable sources. The
adaptive inspection policy alone (Sem.-only, no optimizer) already adds \$18K
(+3.3\%) by skipping \$32K in inspection cost that every inspect-everything
baseline pays regardless. The greedy optimizer, on top of that, adds another
\$55K by routing assets to the right recovery level. Both components contribute
independently. Full demand coverage (DCR 100\%) reflects that with a $r=0.47$
text signal, the optimizer can route every asset to a positive-value path.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{S2.}
The value decomposition in S2 is different and worth stating directly. Text +
adaptive inspection alone (Sem.-only) destroys \$188K relative to Opt-only
because threshold routing without capacity-aware ranking over-processes
low-value assets and misses the ones inspection would catch. The greedy optimizer
recovers \$233K from that hole, leaving a net lift of +\$45K (+2.9\%). XGBoost
outperforms Ours in absolute TRV (\$1,678K vs.\ \$1,610K) because it combines a
calibrated prior ($\phi \approx 0.55$) with full inspection, avoiding any
coverage gaps. At $r=0.32$ text calibration, the inspection savings Ours gains
do not fully compensate for the recoveries it occasionally misses. In a
Part~145-regulated domain, this is the right mode: text directs triage effort;
it does not replace physical inspection authority.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{S3.}
Consumer returns follow the triage pattern. Sem.-only produces strongly negative
TRV ($-\$14$K) because inspection costs exceed recovery value for the assets
threshold routing selects. Ours avoids this by applying the optimizer, landing
at +\$3K (+4.5\%) over Opt-only. Notably, Ours achieves this at slightly
\emph{lower} DCR than Opt-only (93.8\% vs.\ 96.0\%): when phi drops below the
breakeven point, the optimizer correctly scraps the asset rather than processing
it at a loss. Lower DCR here means better selective scrapping, not worse
coverage.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Statistical note.}
Randomness enters only through Monte Carlo yield sampling and seed-varied asset
populations. The $p<0.001$ over 30 seeds measures stability of these effects,
not generalization to held-out datasets or to a different extractor. Every value
in Table~\ref{tab:main} reproduces from
\texttt{python scripts/run\_summary.py --seeds 0-29} in the RLDB.

Three things stand out in Table~\ref{tab:calibration}. First, the gap between

substantially better framework decisions without any architectural change. Second,

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{S1.}
At $r=0.47$ the keyword extractor is substantially above chance on decoupled
data, confirming its vocabulary carries real signal. The full-vocab ceiling
$r=0.73$ is bounded by the note noise imposed during generation
($p_\text{omit}=0.15$, $p_\text{mislabel}=0.25$); organic technician notes
would have different noise characteristics.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{S2.}
ATA codes collapse distinct conditions into near-identical vocabulary, so the
full-vocab reader cannot reliably order them either. The ceiling at 0.54 is lower
than S1 or S3 because the coding system itself destroys ordinal information
before any extractor reads the note.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{S3.}
The full-vocab ceiling (0.74) exceeds S1 (0.73) despite S3 having a weaker
keyword $r$. Consumer return language uses unambiguous categorical extremes:
``dead,'' ``swollen battery,'' ``unopened box.'' The full-vocab reader maps these
cleanly. IT notes carry more mixed-signal phrasing (``fan loud but functional'')
that partially confuses the classifier even when it reads the note correctly.

\subsection{Error Analysis}

Per-asset errors are cases where the framework's realized recovery falls more
than \$50 below what Opt-only would have achieved on the same asset. The dominant
error is overestimation: the extractor assigns $\phi$ higher than warranted,
the system skips inspection, and the asset turns out worse than the text implied.
Error rates are approximate (estimated from sampling): roughly 9\% in S1
(45/500~assets), 24\% in S2 (118/500), near-zero in S3. Total estimated loss
from errors is \$31K (S1), \$70K (S2), and negligible in S3, below the semantic
lift in every case. The S2 rate is the clearest argument for keeping a human in
the loop: keyword matching cannot infer whether a logged repair resolved the
underlying defect.

\subsection{Case Studies}

\noindent\textbf{Case 1 --- Correct skip (S1).}
A note reads ``\textit{Routine decommission. All components seated properly.
No corrosion.}'' The extractor returns $\phi=0.95$, $\sigma=0.95$. The asset
skips inspection and goes directly to $L_2$ disassembly, recovering \$4,200
in components and saving \$200 in inspection cost.

\noindent\textbf{Case 2 --- Correct flag (S1).}
A note reads ``\textit{PSU failure. Visible burn marks on mainboard near power
connector J12. CPU smells burnt.}'' The extractor returns $\phi=0.05$, $\sigma=0.95$.
The very low $\phi$ routes the asset to scrap, avoiding \$70 in wasted disassembly.
Confidence ($\sigma$) and condition ($\phi$) are independent axes; the model is
certain the unit is damaged.

\noindent\textbf{Case 3 --- Overestimation failure (S2).}
An SDR reads ``\textit{Oil on bottom of \#1 engine fan cowl. Replaced filter
and packing.}'' The keyword extractor detects ``replaced'' and emits $\phi=0.98$,
$\sigma=0.85$. The true yield factor is 0.59: the repair addressed a symptom,
not the cause. The overestimation costs \$8,669 against the full-inspection
baseline. A keyword matcher cannot infer whether a logged repair resolved the
underlying defect---this case is why aviation MRO must operate in advisory mode,
with the text signal directing effort rather than replacing inspection authority.

\noindent\textbf{Case 4 --- Categorical triage (S3).}
A review reads ``\textit{It just stopped working after a week. Screen never
turned on.}'' Continuous yield prediction on this text is near-zero, but the
extractor correctly bins the unit as ``likely dead'' and routes it to component
harvesting rather than whole-unit refurbishment. The value is categorical routing
at volume, not a precise yield estimate.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

\noindent\textbf{Capacity.}
At 50\% of baseline capacity, S1 TRV drops 43\% and S2 drops 23\%. When
capacity binds, the engine concentrates on the highest-value assets; the response
is smooth and proportionate.

\noindent\textbf{Confidence thresholds.}
Sweeping $\tau_h$ from 0.5 to 0.95 and $\tau_l$ from 0.2 to 0.75: S1 and S2
favor $\tau_h=0.5$, $\tau_l=0.25$; S3 favors the narrower gap ($\tau_l=0.45$).
Moderate confidence is enough to skip inspection profitably because the expected
cost of a wrong skip (\$200--700) is often less than the certain cost of
universal inspection (\$120--200 per asset).

\noindent\textbf{$\kappa$ sensitivity.}

$\kappa$-invariant, so $\kappa$ does not change which assets get processed or
how they are ranked. We keep $\kappa=10$.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The framework needs no specialized solver: a greedy allocator with MC sampling
and any extractor satisfying a lightweight interface. Adapting to a new industry
means updating the extraction vocabulary and recalibrating the $\phi$-to-yield
mapping, not retraining a model. This makes it accessible to operators without
ML infrastructure.

The strongest use case is where text already exists as part of normal operations
(IT decommission work orders, MRO records) and inspection is the binding resource.
There, even the conservative keyword extractor reduces inspection spend while
recovering competitive TRV. In high-volume, low-margin settings like consumer
returns, the triage framing matters more than the dollar lift: the framework
tells operators which units to route where, without inspecting every one.

\subsection{Limitations}

All simulation data is synthetic, though calibrated from real sources. Organic
text from actual operations may have noise characteristics that differ from the
synthetic notes and would require extractor recalibration before deployment. The
S2 error rate (roughly 24\%) means the framework should not operate autonomously
in safety-critical domains; its role there is to prioritize and triage, not to
replace mandated physical inspection. The optimization is single-period; inventory
carry-over and multi-period dynamics remain open. The $p<0.001$ result measures
simulation stability, not generalization across datasets or extractors.

\section{Conclusion}

The core finding is simple. Unstructured return text, extracted with even a
conservative keyword classifier, contains enough signal to reduce inspection
cost meaningfully while matching or beating the recovery value of an
inspect-every-asset baseline. The mechanism is not a better yield prediction; it
is inspection targeting. By skipping inspection on high-confidence assets and
focusing physical testing on uncertain ones, the framework recovers the same
economic output at lower cost.

The benefit concentrates where text is genuinely informative. In IT
infrastructure decommission, with descriptive technician notes ($r=0.47$), the
keyword extractor delivers a 13.7\% TRV lift and \$32K in inspection savings per
batch. In aviation MRO, where standardized codes limit text signal ($r=0.32$),
the framework is advisory: it saves inspection cost and provides positive TRV
lift over the primary baseline, but a structured-feature baseline with full
inspection still wins on absolute TRV because the text signal is too weak to
compensate for reduced coverage. In consumer electronics, where per-unit
inspection is often economically infeasible, the categorical triage value is the
contribution, not the dollar lift.

The oracle-text calibration bounds (Table~\ref{tab:calibration}) make the
extractor-agnostic property concrete: the same architecture, with a stronger
extractor, would reach $r=0.73$ on S1 and $r=0.74$ on S3. Every result in this
paper is a floor. The gap between the keyword lower bound and the oracle upper
bound shows how much improvement is possible without changing the decision
architecture at all.
